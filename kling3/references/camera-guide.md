# Camera Guide --- Complete Reference

Camera direction is what separates a "generated clip" from a "directed shot." Without it, Kling defaults to static framing. This reference covers every camera tool available to you.

---

## Shot Types

| Shot Type | Abbreviation | What It Shows | Use For |
|-----------|:----------:|---------------|---------|
| Extreme Wide Shot | EWS | Full environment, tiny figures | Establishing location, epic scale |
| Wide Shot | WS | Full environment + characters | Setting context, group scenes |
| Medium Shot | MS | Character from waist up | Conversations, standard storytelling |
| Medium Close-Up | MCU | Head and shoulders | Emotional reactions, dialogue |
| Close-Up | CU | Face fills the frame | Expressions, product detail |
| Extreme Close-Up | ECU | Eyes, lips, texture | Tiny details, tension, intimacy |

---

## Camera Angles

| Angle | Emotional Effect | When to Use |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|
| Eye-level | Neutral, honest, documentary | Normal dialogue, UGC content |
| High angle | Vulnerability, smallness | Character feeling overwhelmed |
| Low angle | Power, dominance, heroism | Product hero shots, reveals |
| Dutch angle | Tension, unease, disorientation | Thriller scenes, something wrong |
| Bird's eye | Scale, patterns, overview | City scenes, crowd shots |
| Worm's eye | Extreme drama, towering scale | Looking up at buildings, epic moments |

---

## Camera Movements

| Movement | What It Does | Prompt Language |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| Static | No movement | "Camera remains still" or "locked shot" |
| Pan | Horizontal rotation (stays in place) | "Camera pans left/right" |
| Tilt | Vertical rotation (stays in place) | "Camera tilts up/down" |
| Dolly | Forward/backward on tracks | "Camera dollies in/out" or "push-in/pull-back" |
| Truck | Lateral sliding movement | "Camera trucks left/right" |
| Crane | Vertical lift with sweep | "Camera cranes up/down" |
| Zoom | Focal length change (not physical move) | "Camera zooms in/out" |
| Handheld | Organic natural shake | "Handheld camera feel" or "slight camera shake" |
| Steadicam | Smooth tracking (follows character) | "Steadicam follows character" |
| Orbit | 360-degree circle around subject | "Camera orbits around subject" |
| Whip pan | Fast horizontal snap | "Quick whip pan to reveal..." |
| Rack focus | Shift between focus planes | "Focus racks from foreground to background" |

---

## Camera Emotional Math

Camera combinations create specific emotional effects. Use these intentionally --- never choose camera angles randomly.

| Camera Combination | Emotional Effect | Example Use |
|-------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| High Angle + Dolly In | Increasing vulnerability | Confession, breakdown scene |
| Low Angle + Static | Power, authority | Hero reveal, product dominance |
| Low Angle + Push-in | Growing intimidation | Dramatic tension building |
| Eye-level + Handheld | Intimacy, authenticity | UGC style, testimonials |
| High Angle + Pull-back | Isolation, loneliness | Emotional moments |
| Dutch Angle + Tracking | Disorientation, urgency | Chase scenes, thriller beats |
| ECU + Slow Dolly In | Building tension | Product reveal, emotional climax |
| Wide + Crane Up | Scale, liberation, resolution | Final reveal, ending shots |
| MCU + Orbit | Examination, showcase | Product 360, character study |
| POV + Handheld | Immersion, empathy | First-person experience, UGC |

### How to Read This Table

Pick the emotion you want the viewer to feel, then use the camera combination that creates it. For example:

- Want the viewer to feel like they're watching a real person's phone video? **Eye-level + Handheld**
- Want the product to feel powerful and premium? **Low Angle + Static**
- Want to build tension before a product reveal? **ECU + Slow Dolly In**
- Want the viewer to feel immersed in the experience? **POV + Handheld**

## Pacing Guide

Match shot duration to the energy of the content. Fast cuts for action, slow holds for emotion.

| Content Energy | Shot Duration | Use For |
|---------------|:------------:|---------|
| Fast / Action | 1-2 seconds | Quick cuts, montages, high energy |
| Medium / Narrative | 3-4 seconds | Standard storytelling, product demos |
| Slow / Emotional | 5+ seconds | Character moments, beauty shots, dialogue |

**Rule:** Never let pacing be accidental. If a shot feels too long, it probably is. If a cut feels jarring, it needs another second.

## POV Shots (Kling Excels at These)

Kling 3.0 is particularly strong at first-person perspective shots. Use these for immersive content.

```
First-person POV from the motorcycle rider. Handlebars and instrument
panel visible in the foreground. Road ahead stretches through mountain
curves. Engine rumble, wind rushing past.
```

POV types that work well:
- Walking POV (exploring a space)
- Car-mounted (driving sequences)
- Drone-style (aerial sweeps)
- Handheld following (behind character)
- Product interaction (hands in frame using product)

### Writing Continuous POV Movement

Use bullet lists under "Camera Movement (Continuous)" for complex sequences:

```
Camera Movement (Continuous):
- Fast forward glide through the front door
- Subtle vertical bob to simulate walking
- Sharp lateral slide between furniture
- Speed ramp forward through a narrow hallway
- Smooth descent down a staircase
- Final push through an open door into sunlight
```

## Speed and Timing Control

You can control motion speed within your prompt:

```
(0-3s) Slow-motion close-up of water droplets hitting the surface.
(4-6s) Normal speed as the character catches the glass.
(7-9s) Slight speed ramp as she lifts the drink to her lips.
```

Speed language Kling understands:
- "Slow-motion" / "96fps slow-mo feel"
- "Normal speed"
- "Speed ramp" (acceleration/deceleration)
- "Deliberate, measured pace"
- "Quick, energetic movement"
- "Frozen moment" (near-stillness)

---

## Edit Transitions Between Scenes

When writing multi-shot prompts, always specify the transition between scenes:

| Transition | Feel | Prompt Language |
|-----------|------|----------------|
| Hard cut | Clean, editorial, professional | "HARD CUT to..." or "CUT TO:" |
| Smash cut | Jarring, dramatic, unexpected | "SMASH CUT to..." |
| Whip cut | Fast, energetic, dynamic | "WHIP CUT to..." |
| Match cut | Elegant, connected, visual rhyme | "Match cut from [A] to [B]" |

Never leave transitions undefined. If you don't specify, Kling may create awkward blends or jarring jumps.
